Instructor Guide — Tech Tools for Homeschool Students

Making Digital Reading Easier

Chrome Reading Mode · Read Aloud · Xodo · Constitution PDF
No Google account needed
Tools: Chrome Reading Mode (sidebar) · Read Aloud (pre-installed) · Xodo at xodo.com · Constitution_Workshop.pdf (on desktop)

Before the workshop — complete these setup steps

  • Tab 1: Pre-load en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Civil_War
  • Tab 2: Pre-load Xodo with Constitution PDF — go to xodo.com → Open PDF → select Constitution_Workshop.pdf from the desktop
  • Set Read Aloud voice: click the Read Aloud icon → voice dropdown → select Google US English → close popup. Setting is saved.
Step-by-step walkthrough
1
Open the Civil War Wikipedia page and show the clutter
⌄

This tab should already be open and pinned. Switch to it now. Point out everything competing for attention — table of contents, sidebar, images, footnotes, navigation boxes, dense columns of text.

en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Civil_War

"Take a look at this page for a moment. Count how many things you can find on here besides the actual article — sidebars, menus, images, links, anything that is not the text itself. This is a typical Wikipedia page — a lot going on. Now watch what happens when I click this one button."

Ask students to call out what they see — "anybody want to name something?" — then proceed to Step 2 without saying another word.

2
Activate Reading Mode and reduce visual distractions
⌄

Click the three dots menu → More Tools → Reading Mode. A sidebar panel opens on the right with clean text only.

"Let me show you how to reduce the visual distractions on any page."

Point to both sides — "Same article. You choose which version your brain works with."

Teach the Reading Mode sidebar controls:

  • Background color — white, sepia, dark, high contrast
  • Font style — serif or sans-serif
  • Font size — increase or decrease
  • Line height — more space between lines — helps students who lose their place
  • Letter spacing — spreads characters apart — reduces crowding for visual tracking difficulties
  • Links — toggle off to reduce visual noise
  • Images — toggle off for text-only experience
"All of these are personal — there is no right setting. Try them until the page feels comfortable to read. The point is that you are in control of how information looks when it comes to you."
Tip: Reading Mode works on most article and text-heavy pages. It may not activate on every website.
3
Use Read Aloud on the Civil War article
⌄

Click the Read Aloud speaker icon in Chrome toolbar. The popup appears and begins reading using the Google US English voice set during setup.

  • Speed slider — start at 1x then bump to 1.2x or 1.5x live so students hear the difference
  • Voice dropdown — already set to Google US English. Students can change at home to find a voice they prefer.
  • Click any word to jump there — demonstrate mid-paragraph. Most useful for re-reading or picking up where they left off.
"You can read along with the highlighting, or just listen while you take notes by hand. You can slow it down for hard vocabulary or speed it up once comfortable. You are in control of how this information gets into your brain."
4
Switch to Xodo and annotate the Constitution PDF
⌄

This tab should already be open with the Constitution PDF loaded. Switch to it now.

"Now we are going to work with a PDF — the format most assigned readings, textbooks, and worksheets come in. We are using the Constitution because most of you have heard of it, and it is dense enough to show why these tools matter."
  • Zoom: Hold Ctrl and scroll up. Reduces visual crowding on dense text.
  • Highlight: Click highlighter icon. Yellow for First Amendment, second color for Second Amendment. Different colors = different categories.
  • Sticky note: Click comment bubble icon → click anywhere → type a question like "What does 'abridging' mean?"

Saving: Click the Download icon in the Xodo toolbar. Annotations are embedded in the saved file.

Tip: No account needed. If a student closes the tab without downloading, annotations are lost.
5
Use Read Aloud on the Constitution PDF inside Xodo
⌄

With the Constitution PDF open in Xodo, click the Read Aloud speaker icon in the Xodo toolbar.

"Now you are using three tools together — a PDF viewer, your own annotations, and text-to-speech — all in one tab. This is how you turn any assigned reading into something you can actually work through."
Alt: If Xodo's reader sounds too robotic, click the Read Aloud Chrome extension icon instead — it uses the better Google voice.

Tools used: Chrome Reading Mode · Read Aloud extension · Xodo (xodo.com) · Constitution PDF

Voice Typing and Writing

Speechnotes · Google Docs · Grammarly
Google account required — bring credentials
Tools: Speechnotes at speechnotes.co · Google Docs · Grammarly (pre-installed)
1 · Speechnotes→ 2 · Google Docs→ 3 · Grammarly
Step-by-step walkthrough
1
Introduce Speechnotes and teach voice commands
⌄

Go to speechnotes.co in Chrome. The notepad opens immediately — no account, no setup. Before students start dictating, walk through the voice commands so they can use them right away.

Speechnotes supports punctuation and basic text commands only — no formatting commands.

What you wantSay thisResult
Period"period".
Comma"comma",
Question mark"question mark"?
Exclamation point"exclamation point"!
Open parenthesis"open parenthesis"(
Close parenthesis"close parenthesis")
Percent sign"percent sign"%
Colon"colon":

Dash"dash"—

New line"new line"line break
"These are the commands you will use while you dictate. Practice saying a full sentence with at least one comma, end by saying period, then say new paragraph and start the next thought."
Tip: Speechnotes works best at normal pace and volume. In a noisy room have students lean slightly toward the mic — not louder than normal.
2
Open the microphone and dictate the Galaxy of Discovery script
⌄

Click the large microphone button at the bottom center of Speechnotes. Click Allow when Chrome asks for microphone permission.

Tip: If the mic doesn't activate: click the microphone icon in the Chrome address bar → Allow.
Alt: If a student's mic isn't working, they can type directly in Speechnotes — Grammarly still activates inside it.

Have students dictate this script using the voice commands they just learned. All formatting is applied in Google Docs after pasting.

Dictate this:

A Galaxy of Discovery new line
A Long Time Ago new line

A long time ago comma in a galaxy far comma far away comma brave explorers dreamed of reaching the stars period For centuries comma humans looked up at the night sky and wondered what secrets the universe was hiding period The dream of space exploration was born in the hearts of scientists comma engineers comma and adventurers who refused to believe the stars were out of reach period new line

The Rebellion Against Gravity new line

In 1957 comma a small metal sphere called Sputnik was launched into orbit comma and the space race began period The rebellion against gravity had officially started period Nations poured their greatest minds and resources into reaching beyond the atmosphere period In 1969 comma the rebellion reached its greatest victory when humans landed on the moon period The rebellion changed everything period new line

The Force of the Future new line

Today the rebellion continues period Space agencies and private companies are launching missions deeper into the galaxy than ever before period Mars comma asteroids comma and distant moons are the new frontier period The force driving exploration forward is the same force that has always guided humanity comma curiosity period The rebellion against gravity will never be complete period
Note: "rebellion" appears 6 times — intentional for the Find and Replace demo in Google Docs.
3
Copy from Speechnotes and paste into Google Docs
⌄

Click the Copy button at the top of Speechnotes. Open a new tab → docs.google.com → sign in → Blank document.

Rename immediately: Galaxy of Discovery – May 2026

Press Ctrl + V to paste.

"You just moved your spoken ideas into a real document. Google Docs is now going to do something Speechnotes cannot — it is going to let us format, organize, and polish this piece."
Note: Google Docs autosaves every change automatically. You will never click Save. Every edit is captured instantly.
4
Apply headings and reveal the Document Outline
⌄
  • Click into "A Galaxy of Discovery" → style dropdown → Heading 1
  • Click into "A Long Time Ago" → Heading 2
  • Click into "The Rebellion Against Gravity" → Heading 2
  • Click into "The Force of the Future" → Heading 2
  • Now go to View → Show Document Outline → four headings appear in left panel immediately
"The outline panel on the left is your navigation map. Click any heading and you jump to that section instantly. For longer documents this is how you move around without scrolling forever."
5
Run Find and Replace
⌄

Press Ctrl + H to open Find and Replace.

  • Find: rebellion
  • Replace with: mission
  • Click Replace All

All six instances replaced instantly across the entire document.

"Six changes in one click. Imagine doing that by hand in a ten-page essay. Find and Replace is one of the most underused tools in writing."
6
View Version History
⌄

Go to File → Version History → See Version History. A timeline panel opens showing every change with timestamps.

"Notice we never once clicked Save. Google Docs saved every single change automatically as you made it. This timeline shows every edit with a timestamp. If you ever delete something by accident or want to go back to an earlier version — it is all here."
Parents: File → Version History → See Version History shows exactly when your student made edits and what changed.
7
Use Grammarly to review the document
⌄

Grammarly activates automatically inside Google Docs.

  • Red — spelling or grammar error
  • Yellow — clarity suggestion
  • Blue — style or tone suggestion

Hover over any underline to see the suggestion. Click to accept or X to dismiss.

"Grammarly does not write for you. It flags things that might be off and you decide whether to change them. You are always in charge of your own words."

Tools used: Speechnotes (speechnotes.co) · Google Docs · Grammarly for Chrome

Organizing Math Work and Graphing

FreeMath · Desmos Graphing · Desmos Scientific · Desmos Geometry
No Google account needed
Tools: freemathapp.org · desmos.com/calculator · desmos.com/scientific · desmos.com/geometry
Step-by-step walkthrough
1
FreeMath — organized step-by-step math work
⌄

Go to freemathapp.org. Under the Students section, click the blue Create button.

"Instead of scratch paper where steps get crossed out and lost, FreeMath keeps every step in order so you can see exactly where something went wrong."

Demo solving 3x + 7 = 22:

  • Type 1 in the Problem Number field — this is Step 1: 3x + 7 = 22
  • Press Enter — the problem copies into a new box below
  • Edit that box to show: 3x = 15
  • Press Enter — copies into a new box below
  • Edit to show the solution: x = 5 — this last box is your answer

Show the math keyboard at the bottom — for inserting ÷ √ fractions and Greek letters.

Show the three dots beside any step — add a step above, change between expression and text mode, or change to an image step.

Tip: Students must save before closing or work is lost. Options: save to device, Google Drive, submit to Google Classroom, or print.
Note: The Draw button allows freehand drawing. The Snap button lets students photograph handwritten work with their webcam and attach it.
2
Desmos Graphing Calculator — equations and tables
⌄

Go to desmos.com/calculator. No account, no install.

Graphing equations:

  • Type y = 2x + 1 → press Enter. A line appears instantly.
  • Type y = x^2 in the next box. A parabola appears automatically.

Changing line color and style:

  • Click the expression box to select it → click the settings icon (colored circle) to the left of the equation
  • Change the color and line style — solid, dotted, or dashed

Table feature with y = 2x + 1:

  • With y = 2x + 1 entered, click + → Table
  • Type x values: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4
  • Desmos fills in y values: 1, 3, 5, 7, 9 automatically based on the equation
  • Points plot on the line already drawn
"The equation, the table, and the graph are all showing you the same relationship three different ways. That is one of the most important ideas in algebra."
Tip: The table auto-fills y values when linked to a linear equation like y = 2x + 1. For other equation types you may need to enter both x and y values manually.
3
Desmos Scientific Calculator
⌄

Go to desmos.com/scientific.

  • Fractions: Type 3/4 → = for decimal. ab/c key keeps it as a fraction.
  • Negative exponents: 2^(-3) gives 1/8
  • Order of operations: (3+5) × 2 − 4 to verify hand calculations.
  • Ans key: Ans + 5 continues from last result without retyping.
  • Trig and log: sin, cos, tan, log, ln — show students where they are.
4
Desmos Geometry — Triangle Angle Sum Theorem
⌄

Go to desmos.com/geometry.

State the theorem first:

"The Triangle Angle Sum Theorem states that the three interior angles of any triangle — no matter its shape or size — will always add up to exactly 180 degrees. We are going to prove that right now visually instead of just taking someone's word for it."
  • Click Polygon → click three points to create a triangle
  • Click Angle → click three points to measure the first angle
  • Repeat for the second and third angles
  • Add the three measurements — they equal 180 degrees
  • Drag any vertex to reshape the triangle — make it extreme
  • Watch the measurements change — add them again — still 180 degrees
"No matter how you reshape this triangle — make it as extreme as you want — those three angles will always add up to 180 degrees. Every triangle that has ever existed follows this rule. That is what a theorem means — it is always true, no exceptions."

Tools used: FreeMath (freemathapp.org) · Desmos Graphing · Desmos Scientific · Desmos Geometry

File Organization and Study Tools

Google Drive · Google Keep · Google Calendar · Browser tools
Google account required — bring credentials
Tools: drive.google.com · keep.google.com · calendar.google.com · Browser tab + bookmark tools
Step-by-step walkthrough
1
Sign in and build a folder structure in Google Drive
⌄

Have students sign in and go to drive.google.com.

"We are going to build a filing system right now that you can use for the next school year. Every assignment goes into the right folder. When you need to find something, you know exactly where it is."
  • Click + New → Folder → name it School – 2026
  • Open it. Create: Language Arts, Math, History, Science
  • Open one subject folder. Create: Assignments, Notes, Resources
Tip: Color-code folders: right-click → Change color. One color per subject.
"Because Drive is connected to your Google account, every document you create or edit is stored in the cloud — not just on that one device. That means if your device crashes, your work is not lost. Whether you are at the library, a co-op class, or a family member's house, you can open your documents from any device with a browser and a Google account. Your work follows you wherever you go."
2
Name files correctly from the start
⌄

Open a new Google Doc. Point to "Untitled document."

"This is the single most important habit. Every file saved as 'Untitled document' is a file that will never be found again. Give it a real name right now."

Naming formula: Subject – Topic – Date

  • History – Chapter 5 Summary – May 2026
  • Science – Photosynthesis Notes – May 2026

Drive search finds content inside Google Docs — even badly named files can be found by searching a word from them.

Version history: File → Version History → See Version History. Every change timestamped automatically — Google Docs autosaves continuously, no manual save needed.

3
Google Keep — checklists, notes, and reminders
⌄

Open keep.google.com.

"Because Keep is connected to your Google account, anything you add on one device shows up instantly on every other device signed into that account. Type a reminder on the desktop and it appears on your phone before you walk out the door. Check off a task on your tablet and your parent sees it updated on their phone in real time. You never have to remember to transfer notes or send yourself an email — it is all just there, everywhere, automatically."
  • Weekly checklist: Click the checklist icon → title it "This Week" → add tasks. Checked items move to the bottom with a strikethrough.
  • Labels: Label icon → Create label → "School." Filter via left sidebar.
  • Reminder: Bell icon → pick date and time. Fires on any signed-in device.
"Think of Keep as your brain dump. Everything you need to remember but might forget goes here. It is always with you."
Tip: Keep icon: click the Keep icon in the right side panel inside Google Docs — the column with the calendar and map icons. The Keep sidebar opens instantly. Notes sync to Keep automatically.
4
Browser organization — no account needed
⌄
  • Pin a tab: Right-click any tab → Pin Tab. Stays at far left even when Chrome closes. Have students pin Drive and Keep right now.
  • Bookmark: Ctrl+D. Create a "School Tools" folder.
  • Bookmark bar: Ctrl+Shift+B to show it.
  • Reopen a closed tab: Ctrl+Shift+T. Press multiple times to reopen more.
  • Tab groups: Right-click any tab → Add to group → New group → name it "School."
Tab groups — share this with students: Tab groups let you organize your browser the way your brain already works. Instead of scrolling through 30 open tabs, you create labeled, color-coded groups — one per subject or goal. Collapse what you are not using. Expand what you need. You can organize by outcome — "Complete History Essay by Friday" — not just by subject. That habit of thinking in deadlines and goals is exactly what college will require. One click opens your workspace. One click puts it away.
Tip: Have every student pin Drive and Keep before they leave. Ten seconds now makes a real practical difference at home.
5
Google Calendar — tracking assignments and deadlines
⌄

Open calendar.google.com.

"Any deadline or event you add on one device appears on every device instantly. Set a reminder on the desktop and it fires on your phone. Your parent can add an assignment deadline from their phone and you see it on your device without anyone sending a text or email."
  • Create an event: Click any date → type assignment name → Save.
  • Color-code by subject: Click event → edit → change the color dot.
  • Subject calendar: Click + next to "Other calendars" → Create new calendar → "Math." Toggle on and off independently.
  • Reminder: Add notification → 1 day before. Fires on any signed-in device.
"This is how you track your own deadlines without relying on someone else to remind you. That is a real skill worth building now."

Tools used: Google Drive · Google Keep · Google Calendar · Browser tab + bookmark tools
